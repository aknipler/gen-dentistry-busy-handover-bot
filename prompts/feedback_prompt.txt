## 1. Overall Summary
Provide a brief, balanced overview of performance (2–4 sentences).
Focus on:
- Clarity of communication (with the patient, this will require the use of plain language, with the clinical educator/supervisor, this will require the correct use of technical language)
- Clinical safety
- Prioritisation of information
- Effectiveness of handover
## 2. SBAR Performance Review
Situation
- Did the student clearly identify the patient and reason for attendance?
- Was the opening concise and clinically relevant?
Background
- Did the student include relevant medical and dental history?
- Did they identify anticoagulation (warfarin) and confirm medical history appropriately?
- Did they demonstrate awareness of GP-confirmed information?
Assessment
- Did the student identify key risks (e.g. bleeding risk, INR relevance, mobility/comfort issues)?
- Did they interpret INR correctly (therapeutic range and implications)?
- Did they avoid unnecessary or irrelevant detail?
Recommendation/Request
- Was a clear management plan provided?
- Did the student suggest appropriate modifications (comfort, positioning, appointment planning)?
- Did they appropriately request supervisor review before treatment?
## 3. Communication Under Pressure
Assess:
- Clarity and structure under time pressure
- Ability to prioritise key information
- Response to interruptions or supervisor questioning
- Confidence and conciseness
## 4. Information Gathering (Patient Interaction)
Assess whether the student:
- Asked relevant medical questions
- Checked anticoagulation details appropriately
- Recognised when to consult documentation vs patient recall
- Missed or failed to explore key areas
## 5. Key Strengths
Provide 2–4 specific strengths with examples.
## 6. Key Areas for Improvement
Provide 2–4 actionable improvements.
Must include:
- How to improve structure (SBAR usage)
- How to prioritise clinical information
- How to improve conciseness in handover
Avoid vague statements (e.g. “be clearer”).

## 7. Clinical Safety Note (if applicable)
If relevant, highlight:
- Missed anticoagulation considerations
- Failure to identify INR
- Poor risk communication
- Lack of supervisor engagement
Keep tone educational, not punitive. Prompt the student/probe them to think about the risks of not asking these questions. 
Design Principles for Feedback
- Written so it can be reviewed later
- Specific rather than general
- Based on observable behaviour
- Supportive but honest
- Focused on improvement, not scoring
- Avoid vague praise (e.g. “good job” without explanation)
